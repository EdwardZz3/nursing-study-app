Unit One: An Overview of Human Growth and
Development
Objectives
By the end of this unit, students should be able to:
Define growth, maturation, and development.
Differentiate between growth and development.
Identify types, importance, and principles of growth and development.
Recognize patterns and stages of growth and development.
Enumerate factors influencing growth and development.
Introduction
Growth  and  development  are  multifaceted  processes  influenced  by  a  variety  of  factors—some
modifiable, others not. Knowledge of these influences is crucial for healthcare providers, especially
nurses, to guide parents in fostering optimal child growth and development.
Core Definitions
Growth:  Quantitative increase in physical size due to cell multiplication in organs; measured by
height, weight, head, and chest circumference.
Maturation:  Sequential biological changes that enable new abilities, reflecting the orderly
development of the child.
Development:  Qualitative progression in skills, functions, and social relations; a lifelong continuous
process from conception to maturity.•

Types of Growth and Development
Physical Growth:  Monitored via head circumference, thoracic diameter, weight, and height.
Physiological Growth:  Assessed through vital signs (temperature, pulse, respiration, blood
pressure).
Motor Development:  Includes milestones like sitting, standing, running, and fine muscle use.
Mental/Intellectual Development:  Problem-solving abilities.
Social Development:  Cultural training and socialization.
Emotional/Personality Development:  Needs for trust, love, and security.
Importance of Studying Growth and Development
Anticipate age-appropriate behaviors.
Understand child behavior and developmental norms.
Diagnose nutritional and deficiency disorders.
Plan comprehensive patient care.
Assist parents in adjusting the environment to support optimal growth.
Principles of Development
Continuous and Sequential:  Each stage builds on the previous one.
Predictable:  Occurs in a predetermined order influenced by genetics and environment.
Maturation and Learning Dependent:  Requires biological readiness and experience.•

Early Sensitivity:  Early development is more critical to long-term outcomes.
Individual Variation:  Individual differences exist in the rate, but not the sequence.
Patterns of Growth and Development
Cephalocaudal:  Growth progresses from the head downward.
Proximodistal:  Development progresses from the midline out to the extremities.
General to Specific:  Progression moves from gross motor skills to fine motor skills.

Factors Influencing Growth and Development
Prenatal Factors
Hereditary/Genetics:  Parental traits influence physical characteristics (e.g., height).
Race/Nationality:  Variations in growth patterns among different races.4.

Maternal Diseases:  Conditions like congenital rubella or metabolic disorders.
Drugs:  Teratogenic effects (e.g., amphetamines causing heart defects).
Nutrition:  Severe maternal malnutrition can severely impair fetal development.
Postnatal Factors: External
Environment:  Climate impacts growth rates; hot climates promote faster growth.
Seasonal Variation:  Height grows faster in spring/summer; weight increases in autumn/winter.
Oxygen Concentration:  Chronic hypoxia leads to a smaller physical size.
Environmental Hazards:  Trauma (e.g., poliomyelitis) and socioeconomic status.
Postnatal Factors: Internal
Endocrine System:  Hormones like growth hormone, thyroid hormone, and androgens stimulate
growth.
Gender:  Boys typically have greater muscle strength and activity levels.
Psychological State:  Emotional and maternal deprivation negatively affect weight and growth.
Health Status:  Diseases, drugs, and overall nutrition affect long-term development.
Nurse’s Assessment of Growth and Development
Assessing Growth:  Measuring head and chest circumference, weight, height, and observing
teeth eruption.
Assessing Development:  Observing motor, social, mental, and emotional milestones, as well
as sensory capabilities.•
