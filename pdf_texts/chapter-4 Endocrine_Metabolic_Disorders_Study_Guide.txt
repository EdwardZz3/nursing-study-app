UNIT THREE: ENDOCRINE AND
METABOLIC DISORDERS
I. Diabetes Mellitus (DM)
Definition:  Diabetes Mellitus is a chronic condition characterized by insufficient insulin
production by the pancreas or ineffective insulin utilization, causing hyperglycemia. It
disrupts carbohydrate, protein, and fat metabolism.
Types:
Type 1 Diabetes:  Autoimmune destruction of pancreatic beta cells results in insulin
deficiency. Also called insulin-dependent or juvenile diabetes. The autoimmune cause
is unknown.
Type 2 Diabetes:  Characterized initially by insulin resistance, later possibly insulin
deficiency. Known as non-insulin-dependent or adult-onset diabetes. Mainly caused
by obesity and physical inactivity.
Causes:
Type 1:  Autoimmune destruction or idiopathic.
Type 2:  Lifestyle and genetic factors including obesity, sedentary behavior, diet,
stress, urban living, tobacco smoking, family history, growth spurts, and parity.
Signs and Symptoms:
Polyuria, polydipsia, polyphagia
Weight loss, fatigue, weakness
Blurred vision, headache
Slow wound healing, numbness or tingling in extremities
Frequent infections
Stunted growth and coma in neglected cases
Diagnosis:
Clinical history and physical exam•

Laboratory tests: fasting blood glucose, postprandial glucose, glucose curve, random
glucose, and HbA1C
Management:
Diet:  Carbohydrates (45-50%), proteins (20%), fats (30-35%), with frequent meals (6/
day)
Insulin therapy:  Aims to save life, maintain normal glucose, and minimize
complications
Exercise:  Improves glucose tolerance, reduces insulin needs, controls weight, and
minimizes complications. Avoid hard exercise; carry sweets to prevent hypoglycemia.
Complications:
Hypoglycemia, diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA)
Dental decay, retinopathy, nephropathy, neuropathy
Diabetic foot ulcers, cardiovascular diseases
Insulin resistance, allergic reactions, injection site complications
Prevention:
Type 1:  No known prevention
Type 2:  Maintain healthy weight, physical activity, balanced diet, stop smoking, stress
management
Nursing Care:
Glycemic control through medication, diet, and lifestyle
Education on management, insulin administration, and complication recognition
Prevention of complications (cardiovascular, nephropathy, etc.)
Foot care and hygiene
Regular monitoring and follow-up
II. Congenital Hypothyroidism
Definition:  A  disorder  present  from  birth  where  the  thyroid  gland  fails  to  produce
sufficient thyroid hormone, causing growth and mental development delays.•

Causes:
Common:
Thyroid dysgenesis (ectopic, hypoplasia, agenesis)
Thyroid dyshormonogenesis (normal location with defective hormone synthesis)
Pituitary disease causing TSH deficiency
Less common:
Maternal thyroid blocking antibodies
Maternal medications (anti-thyroid drugs)
Failure of brain signaling to thyroid
Signs and Symptoms:
Often  asymptomatic  at  birth;  newborn  screening  is  critical.  Symptoms  developing  in
infancy include:
Puffy face, macroglossia, large fontanels
Hoarse cry, umbilical hernia, constipation
Prolonged jaundice, hypotonia, lethargy
Feeding difficulties, cold extremities, dry skin
Growth delays, delayed bone and tooth development
Brain development retardation leading to intellectual disability
Diagnosis:
Newborn screening (T4 and TSH levels)
Detailed history and physical exam
Assessment of pituitary hormone deficiencies and congenital malformations
Treatment:
Levothyroxine replacement (dose varies by age, e.g., newborns 10-15 μg/kg/day)
Vitamin D supplementation to prevent rickets
Complications:
Permanent cognitive impairment, growth delays, hearing loss if untreated
Nursing Interventions:
Monitor weight, appetite, vitals, bowel patterns•

III. Hyperthyroidism (Goiter)
Definition:  Overproduction of thyroid hormones leading to excessive metabolic activity.
Risk Factors:
More common in adolescent females
Family history of thyroid or autoimmune disease
Maternal Graves' disease history
Causes:
Graves' disease (autoimmune) most common
Excess thyroid hormone medication
Excess dietary iodine
Iodine deficiency
Thyroid nodules or cancer
Thyroiditis (inflammation)
Pregnancy-related hormonal effects
Classification:
Physiological goiter (puberty, pregnancy)
Endemic goiter (iodine deficient areas)
Toxic goiter/thyrotoxicosis (accelerated metabolism and hormone production)
Signs and Symptoms:
Enlarged thyroid, weight loss, increased metabolism•

Tachycardia, hypertension
Exophthalmos (eye bulging)
Tremors, heat intolerance, sweating
Anxiety, irritability, restlessness, insomnia
Gastrointestinal hypermotility (loose stools)
Soft, warm skin; fatigue; increased appetite
In girls, menstrual irregularities
Diagnosis:
Physical exam, thyroid function tests
Ultrasound, biopsy, antibody testing
Imaging (CT/MRI) to assess goiter size and spread
Treatment:
Anti-thyroid medications to reduce hormone production
Radioactive iodine therapy for permanent treatment
Surgery (thyroidectomy) to remove gland partially or totally
Nursing Interventions:
Provide rest, monitor vital signs
Administer medications as prescribed
Prepare for radioactive iodine or surgery
Monitor for drug toxicity (fever, headache, GI symptoms, joint stiffness)
Educate about disease, medication, and complications•
