Unit Eleven: Congenital Anomalies
Definition and Etiology
Congenital anomalies are structural or functional abnormalities present at birth, arising from
genetic,  environmental,  or  unknown  causes.  Predisposing  factors  include  hereditary  defects,
maternal infections (e.g., rubella), teratogens, anticonvulsant therapy, and maternal diabetes.
Prevention:
Preconception immunization (MMR vaccine)
Premarital and genetic counseling
Optimal antenatal care including fetal anomaly screening
Avoidance of harmful exposures during pregnancy
Healthy lifestyle (nutrition, avoidance of toxins)
Nursing Role in Surgical Correction:
Preoperative assessment includes IV line insertion, correction of anemia and electrolyte
imbalances, prophylactic antibiotics, parental education, and support. Postoperative care
focuses on ventilation, cardiac function, thermoregulation, fluid management, wound care,
pain control, and family education. 
Central Nervous System Anomalies
Spina Bifida
Definition:  Defect  in  vertebral  closure  with  types  including  occulta,  meningocele,
myelomeningocele, and encephalocele. Severity varies with neurologic involvement.
Clinical  Features:  Occulta  often  asymptomatic;  meningocele  and  myelomeningocele  involve
neurologic deficits and urinary/bowel dysfunction.
Treatment:  Surgical closure shortly after birth, physical therapy, incontinence management.
Prevention:  Folic acid supplementation (minimum 0.4 mg daily preconception and during early
pregnancy; 4 mg if history of spina bifida).•

Nursing Care:
Prevent infection and trauma to lesion, positioning, monitoring for increased intracranial
pressure (ICP), skin care, family teaching. 
Hydrocephalus
Definition:  CSF  accumulation  due  to  imbalance  in  production  and  absorption,  causing
ventricular dilation and ICP increase.
Clinical Signs:  Abnormal head growth, bulging fontanel, setting sun sign, headaches, ataxia.
Nursing Care:
Pre/postoperative monitoring of head circumference, vital signs, positioning, infection
prevention, family education. 
Gastrointestinal Anomalies
Cleft Lip and Palate:
Failure of maxillary and premaxillary processes to fuse, causing feeding difficulties and speech
impairment.  Surgical  repair  staged  with  lip  closure  before  palate.  Nursing  care  focuses  on
feeding support, postoperative care to protect suture site, and long-term speech and hearing
support.
Esophageal Atresia with Tracheoesophageal Fistula:
Discontinuity  of  esophagus  with  abnormal  connection  to  trachea.  Presents  with  excessive
drooling, choking, cyanosis on feeding. Requires surgical repair.
Diaphragmatic Hernia:
Herniation of abdominal contents into thorax causing respiratory distress. Surgical emergency
with supportive respiratory care preoperatively.
Hirschsprung Disease:
Congenital absence of ganglion cells in colon causing motility disorder and obstruction. Presents
with  delayed  meconium  passage,  abdominal  distension,  constipation.  Managed  by  surgical
resection.
Hypertrophic Pyloric Stenosis:
Hypertrophy  of  pyloric  muscle  causing  gastric  outlet  obstruction;  presents  with  projectile
nonbilious vomiting and palpable mass. Requires surgical pyloromyotomy.

Intussusception:
Telescoping of bowel causing obstruction and ischemia. Presents with intermittent colicky pain,
currant jelly stools, and palpable mass. Diagnosed by barium enema and treated with hydrostatic
reduction or surgery.
Anorectal Malformations:
Includes  imperforate  anus  and  fistulas;  diagnosed  by  absence  of  anal  opening  and  treated
surgically.
Genitourinary Anomalies
Hypospadias and Epispadias:
Abnormal urethral openings; surgical correction needed, circumcision deferred until after repair.
Undescended Testes:
Failure of testicular descent; surgery recommended by age 4-5 to prevent infertility and injury.
Unit Twelve: Urinary System Disorders
Kidney Function
Key roles include waste excretion, electrolyte and water regulation, hormone production (renin,
erythropoietin), and vitamin D metabolism.
Nephrotic Syndrome
Definition:
Increased glomerular permeability causing massive proteinuria, hypoalbuminemia, salt and
water retention, and edema. 
Incidence:
Common in ages 2-5; more boys affected.
Types:
Primary (idiopathic), secondary (systemic diseases), congenital (genetic).
