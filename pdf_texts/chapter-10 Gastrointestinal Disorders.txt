Gastrointestinal Disorders: Gastroenteritis
Definition and Epidemiology:
Gastroenteritis is an acute inflammation of the stomach and intestines, characterized
primarily by vomiting and diarrhea. It is highly prevalent in pediatric populations globally
and is a leading cause of morbidity and mortality among children. Viral agents, especially
rotavirus, are the predominant causative factors in both developed and developing
countries. 
Mode of Transmission:
The  primary  modes  include  the  fecal-oral  route,  ingestion  of  contaminated  food  and  water,
transmission in daycare or school settings, and potentially respiratory tract inoculation.
Types and Clinical Manifestations:
Bacterial Gastroenteritis:  Typically presents with fever above 38.5°C  and severe or bloody
diarrhea. Common pathogens include Shigella , Salmonella , E. coli , Campylobacter , and 
Yersinia enterocolitica . Stool characteristics help guide diagnosis, but stool culture is
definitive.
Viral Gastroenteritis:  Usually features lower-grade fever ( < 38°C ) and watery, non-bloody
diarrhea. Rotavirus is the most common agent.
Parasitic Enteritis:  Manifestations depend on the parasite; for example, Giardia lamblia
causes watery, foul-smelling diarrhea without fever; amoebiasis may cause bloody diarrhea
without fever.•

Red Flag Symptoms:
Altered sensorium
Bilious or bloody vomiting
Cyanosis
Excessive irritability or inconsolable crying
Petechial rash
Low body weight
Poor peripheral perfusion
Rapid breathing
Temperature ≥ 40°C
Complications:
Severe cases, especially bacterial, can lead to metabolic complications (dehydration, acidosis,
electrolyte  imbalances,  acute  renal  failure),  cardiovascular  shock,  neurologic  complications
(convulsions,  coma),  hematologic  bleeding,  and  digestive  issues  such  as  persistent  diarrhea
causing malabsorption and malnutrition.
Prevention:
Key strategies include rigorous handwashing, rotavirus vaccination (oral pentavalent vaccine at
2,  4,  and  6  months),  safe  diaper  handling,  and  water  purification  methods  (boiling  or
chlorination).
Management:
Mild to moderate cases are managed at home focusing on dehydration prevention, diet,
and symptomatic treatment.
Severe cases require hospitalization with intravenous fluids for shock and electrolyte
correction, antibiotics for persistent high fever or severe bacterial infection, and
treatment of complications.
Nursing care emphasizes hydration with oral rehydration solution (ORS), careful feeding
practices (small frequent meals during and post-diarrhea), and monitoring for signs of
overhydration.
Drug Therapy:
Antibiotics are generally ineffective for diarrhea and may prolong illness or cause resistance.
Anti-diarrheal  and  anti-motility  drugs  are  discouraged.  Prevention  focuses  on  breastfeeding,
appropriate weaning, hygiene, and measles vaccination.•

Typhoid Fever
Definition:
Typhoid fever is a systemic infection caused by Salmonella typhi , primarily affecting
intestinal lymphoid tissue, especially Peyer's patches, and capable of bacteremia. 
Transmission and Incubation:
Spread via contaminated food, water, or direct contact with carriers; incubation ranges from 3
days to 1 month, commonly 8-14 days.
Clinical Manifestations:
Week 1:  Frontal headache, intermittent high fever (up to 40°C ), epistaxis, bronchitis,
abdominal distension, diarrhea or constipation, slow pulse, splenomegaly, trunk rash, stupor.
Week 2:  Abdominal symptoms worsen, persistent fever, enlarged spleen.
Week 3:  Prostration, weakness, fatigue, circulatory failure.
Complications:
Intestinal hemorrhage due to vessel erosion
Intestinal perforation causing acute abdomen and shock
Metearism (abdominal gas distension causing cardiac pressure)
Acute myocarditis from endotoxins leading to arrhythmias
Diagnosis:
Blood cultures in the first week, followed by stool cultures. Isolation is recommended until stool
cultures are negative (typically 3 consecutive weekly cultures).
Prevention:
Good hygiene, sanitary waste disposal, drinking treated water, consuming pasteurized dairy,
avoiding raw shellfish, vaccination for travelers or contacts, and food/water safety practices
("boil it, cook it, peel it, or forget it").
Management:
Supportive care includes hydration, antipyretics, and nutrition. Chloramphenicol IV for 3 weeks
is the main antibiotic treatment. Corticosteroids are indicated in severe toxemia.•

Nursing Care:
Assessment focuses on travel history and febrile illness symptoms; nursing diagnoses
include fluid volume deficit, imbalanced nutrition, acute pain, activity intolerance, and
hyperthermia. Interventions encompass assistance with ADLs, hydration encouragement,
nutrition support, pain and temperature management, and family education. Evaluation
confirms goals such as fluid balance and symptom control. 
Diarrhea
Definition:
Passage of three or more loose or liquid stools per day, distinct from frequent formed stools. 
Causes:
Infective: bacterial ( E. coli , Salmonella , Shigella ), viral (rotavirus), protozoal ( Giardia ), fungal.
Non-infective: dietary causes (overfeeding, unsuitable formula), malabsorption, allergies,
endocrine disorders.
Classification by Duration:
Acute (< 1 month)
Persistent (more than 2 weeks)
Chronic (> 1 month), often related to malabsorption or systemic illness
Types:
Acute watery diarrhea (80%): self-limited, watery stools, dehydration risk
Dysentery (5-10%): bloody stools, intestinal mucosa damage
Persistent diarrhea: prolonged post-infection diarrhea with weight loss
Chronic diarrhea: linked to malabsorption, allergies, IBD
Chronic nonspecific diarrhea: common in toddlers, no malnutrition or infection
Intractable diarrhea of infancy: prolonged, refractory diarrhea in infants
Clinical Features:
Includes diarrhea, vomiting, abdominal colic/distension, tenesmus, fever, irritability, dehydration
signs.•

Stool Characteristics Indicative of Pathogens:
Watery, colorless: rotavirus, E. coli
Rice water: Vibrio cholera
Mucus, pus, blood: Shigella , Salmonella
Frothy, pale: Giardia
Mucus, blood, pus with tenesmus: E. histolytica
Complications:
Metabolic: dehydration, electrolyte imbalances (hypo/hypernatremia, hypocalcemia,
hypokalemia), acid-base disturbances
CNS: convulsions, meningitis, cerebral thrombosis
Cardiovascular: shock
Pulmonary: infections, ARDS
Renal: acute failure, hemolytic uremic syndrome
GI: ileus, rectal prolapse, obstruction
Hematologic: disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC)
Nutritional: predisposition to malnutrition
Assessment:
Includes history, weight monitoring, vital signs, intake/output, thirst, skin turgor, edema.
Management:
ORS is the mainstay; administered frequently in small amounts.
Antibiotics generally avoided except for specific infections.
Nutrition maintained or resumed early.
Prevention: vaccination (rotavirus), breastfeeding, hygiene, safe water and food, measles
vaccination.

Causes:
Fever, diarrhea, vomiting, poor intake, environmental heat, burns, blood loss, cystic fibrosis.
Clinical Manifestations:
Thirst,  sunken  eyes/fontanelle,  dry  mucous  membranes,  loss  of  skin  elasticity,  oliguria,
irritability/lethargy, shock in severe cases.
Classification:
Mild: 2-4% weight loss, thirst only
Moderate: 5-9% weight loss, dehydration signs present
Severe: ≥10% weight loss, shock signs
Types:
Isotonic: equal loss of water and sodium; serum Na ≈ 130-150  mEq/L
Hypotonic: more sodium loss; serum Na < 130  mEq/L; risk of cerebral edema
Hypertonic: more water loss; serum Na > 150  mEq/L; risk of brain cell dehydration
Assessment:
History, weight, physical exam for dehydration signs, electrolyte and renal function tests.
Treatment:
Plan A:  Home rehydration with ORS for mild dehydration
Plan B:  Outpatient ORS with medical supervision for moderate dehydration
Plan C:  Hospital IV fluids for severe dehydration/shock
Feeding is continued or resumed promptly; breastfeeding is never stopped.
Unit Ten: Malignant Neoplasm
Overview of Childhood Cancer
Childhood cancers vary by type and location, often differing from adult cancers in behavior and
treatment response. They involve blood, lymphatics, CNS, kidneys, and other tissues. Most have
no known cause.•
