Intussusception:
Telescoping of bowel causing obstruction and ischemia. Presents with intermittent colicky pain,
currant jelly stools, and palpable mass. Diagnosed by barium enema and treated with hydrostatic
reduction or surgery.
Anorectal Malformations:
Includes  imperforate  anus  and  fistulas;  diagnosed  by  absence  of  anal  opening  and  treated
surgically.
Genitourinary Anomalies
Hypospadias and Epispadias:
Abnormal urethral openings; surgical correction needed, circumcision deferred until after repair.
Undescended Testes:
Failure of testicular descent; surgery recommended by age 4-5 to prevent infertility and injury.
Unit Twelve: Urinary System Disorders
Kidney Function
Key roles include waste excretion, electrolyte and water regulation, hormone production (renin,
erythropoietin), and vitamin D metabolism.
Nephrotic Syndrome
Definition:
Increased glomerular permeability causing massive proteinuria, hypoalbuminemia, salt and
water retention, and edema. 
Incidence:
Common in ages 2-5; more boys affected.
Types:
Primary (idiopathic), secondary (systemic diseases), congenital (genetic).
11
Clinical Manifestations:
Pitting edema, ascites, pleural effusion, weight gain, pallor, anorexia, decreased urine output.
Diagnosis:
Proteinuria > 2g/24 hrs, renal biopsy.
Management:
Corticosteroids, diuretics, sodium restriction, activity as tolerated, home monitoring.
Complications:
Acute kidney failure, infections, growth retardation.
Nursing:
Monitor edema, weight, vitals, skin integrity, nutrition, educate caregivers. 
Acute Glomerulonephritis
Definition:
Immune-mediated glomerular inflammation, often post-streptococcal infection. 
Clinical Features:
Hematuria, oliguria, hypertension, edema, malaise, headache, fever.
Diagnosis:
Urinalysis with hematuria and proteinuria, blood tests, throat culture.
Management:
Activity restriction, antibiotics if needed, symptomatic care.
Nursing:
Monitor urine output, BP, weight, infection prevention. 
Urinary Tract Infection (UTI)
Definition:
Bacterial infection of urinary tract, more common in girls and uncircumcised males. 
12
Symptoms:
Fever, abdominal pain, dysuria, urgency, incontinence, foul-smelling urine.
Diagnosis:
Urine analysis and culture, renal ultrasound if recurrent.
Treatment:
Antibiotics, increased fluids.
Prevention:
Hygiene, avoiding tight clothes, frequent voiding, managing constipation.
Renal Failure
Types:
Acute Renal Failure (ARF): sudden, often reversible
Chronic Renal Failure (CRF): progressive, irreversible
Causes:
Prerenal (low blood flow), intrarenal (tissue damage), postrenal (obstruction).
Phases of ARF:
Oliguric (low urine output, electrolyte imbalances)
Diuretic (increased urine, electrolyte losses)
Recovery
Clinical Manifestations:
Multisystem effects including cardiovascular (anemia, HTN), pulmonary (edema), GI (nausea),
neuromuscular (weakness), metabolic (acidosis), and psychosocial.
Diagnosis:
Blood and urine tests, imaging, biopsy.
Treatment:
Hospitalization, fluid/electrolyte management, medications, dialysis, transplantation.
Nursing:
Monitor fluid status, nutrition, infection risk, teach family. •
