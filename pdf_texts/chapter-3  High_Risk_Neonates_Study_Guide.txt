UNIT TWO: HIGH-RISK NEONATES
Definition
A high-risk neonate is a newborn with an increased likelihood of morbidity and mortality
due to prenatal, perinatal, or postnatal factors.
Predisposing Factors
Maternal:  diabetes, hypertension, labor complications.
Neonatal:  prematurity, low birth weight, low Apgar, congenital anomalies.
Nursing Care
Frequent assessment, monitoring vital signs electronically.
Minimal handling.
Monitor feeding reflexes, color, temperature.
Record fluid intake and weight.
Document observations carefully.
Classification of High-Risk Neonates
Prematurity.
Physiologic factors (e.g., jaundice).
Respiratory distress syndrome.
Infant of diabetic mother.
Neonatal sepsis.
Neonatal hypothermia.•

Premature Infants
Gestational Age and Physical Maturity Assessment
Gestational age determined by last menstrual period, ultrasound, physical and
neuromuscular maturity (Ballard score).
Categories: extremely preterm (<28 weeks), very preterm (28–31 weeks), moderately
preterm (32–33 weeks), late preterm (34–36 weeks).
Physical signs include skin texture, lanugo, plantar creases, breast size, ear cartilage,
genital development.
Neuromuscular signs: posture, wrist flexibility, arm recoil, popliteal angle, scarf sign,
heel-to-ear.
Classification by Birth Weight
ELBW < 1000 g.
VLBW 1000-1499 g.
LBW 1500-2500 g.
Causes of Prematurity
Multiple births, maternal infections, toxemia, hemorrhage, malnutrition, uterine
anomalies, substance abuse, trauma.
Physical Characteristics
Small, thin, large head relative to body.
Wrinkled red skin with lanugo.
Soft ears, small thorax, protruding abdomen.
Minimal creases on palms and soles.
Handicaps
Poor thermoregulation due to large surface area and immature heat centers.
Respiratory difficulties from immature lungs, weak muscles, poor cough reflex.
Immature immune system.
Biochemical deficits: liver immaturity causing bleeding and jaundice; kidney inability to
concentrate urine; nutritional deficits.•

Complications
Intraventricular hemorrhage.
Hyaline membrane disease.
Hypoglycemia.
Apnea.
Necrotizing enterocolitis.
Retinopathy of prematurity.
Kernicterus.
Prevention
Early and adequate prenatal care.
Healthy maternal lifestyle.
Diagnosis
Continuous monitoring of vital signs.
Blood tests for calcium, glucose, bilirubin.
Imaging for brain, heart, abdomen.
Eye examinations.
Care
Incubator with controlled temperature (32-36°C) and humidity (60-70%).
Oxygen therapy cautiously to prevent retinopathy.
Vital sign monitoring.
Feeding support via IV and NG tube initially.
Phototherapy for jaundice.
Blood transfusions as needed.
Medications: surfactant, antibiotics, diuretics.
Neonatal Jaundice
Pathophysiology
Excess bilirubin (unconjugated and conjugated forms).
Immature liver unable to metabolize bilirubin efficiently.•

Types
Physiological jaundice:  appears day 2-3; resolves within two weeks.
Breast milk jaundice:  occurs days 7-14; due to breast milk substances inhibiting
bilirubin metabolism.
Pathological jaundice:  appears within first 24 hours; caused by hemolysis,
infections, biliary obstruction.
Complications
Kernicterus: bilirubin encephalopathy causing irreversible brain damage.
Management
Early feeding to promote bilirubin excretion.
Phototherapy using blue or white light.
Exchange transfusion for severe cases.
Respiratory Distress Syndrome (RDS)
Causes
Prematurity-related surfactant deficiency.
Risk factors: cesarean delivery, multiple birth, hypothermia, perinatal asphyxia.
Clinical Signs
Tachypnea (>60 breaths/min), cyanosis, nasal flaring, chest retractions, grunting,
apnea.
Treatment
Artificial surfactant via endotracheal tube.
Oxygen therapy (nasal cannula or oxygen hood).
CPAP to keep alveoli open.
Mechanical ventilation if necessary.
Nursing Care
Continuous monitoring of oxygen saturation and blood gases.
Suctioning as needed.
Mouth care for NPO neonates.•

Infant of Diabetic Mother
Characteristics
Large for gestational age.
Organomegaly (liver, heart).
Risk of hypoglycemia post-birth due to hyperinsulinemia.
Symptoms
Cyanosis, poor feeding, tremors.
Prevention and Treatment
Maternal diabetes control.
Neonatal blood glucose monitoring.
Early feeding or IV glucose.
Supportive respiratory care.
Nursing Care
Monitor hypoglycemia signs.
Feed frequently.
Monitor glucose levels regularly.
Neonatal Sepsis
Definition
Systemic infection in newborn causing multisystem involvement.
Causes
Maternal infections, prolonged rupture of membranes, difficult delivery.
Classification
Early onset (<3 days):  maternal source.
Late onset (1-3 weeks):  environmental source.•

Risk Factors
Prematurity, low birth weight, poor hygiene, instrumentation.
Clinical Manifestations
Temperature instability.
Respiratory distress.
CNS signs: lethargy, irritability.
GI symptoms: feeding intolerance, vomiting.
Hematologic: jaundice, pallor.
Complications
Septic shock, meningitis, respiratory failure.
Diagnosis
CBC, blood cultures, lumbar puncture, CRP, imaging.
Treatment
Empiric antibiotics (ampicillin + gentamicin).
Supportive care for respiratory, cardiovascular, hematologic functions.
Nursing Care
Infection control practices.
Strict hygiene and isolation as needed.
Monitor for antibiotic side effects.
Prevent complications.
Neonatal Hypothermia
Definition
Abnormally low body temperature due to excessive heat loss.
Causes
Environmental exposure.
Immature thermoregulation.•

Illness.
Risk Factors
Prematurity, low Apgar, cesarean delivery.
Classification
Mild:  (35-36.3°C).
Moderate:  (32-34.9°C).
Severe:  (<32°C).
Signs and Symptoms
Cool, pale skin.
Lethargy, poor feeding.
Respiratory distress.
Hypoglycemia.
Complications
Hypoxia, acidosis, clotting disorders.
Prevention ("Warm Chain")
Warm delivery room.
Immediate drying.
Skin-to-skin contact.
Early breastfeeding.
Delayed bathing/weighing.
Appropriate clothing.
Warm transportation.
Treatment
Mild:  skin-to-skin, warm environment.
Moderate:  radiant heaters, incubator.
Severe:  incubator with controlled warming.
Nursing Assessment and Intervention
Monitor temperature, vital signs.•
