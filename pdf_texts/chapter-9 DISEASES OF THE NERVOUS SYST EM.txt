DISEASES OF THE NERVOUS SYSTEM
1. Cerebral Palsy (CP)
Definition:  Group of disorders affecting movement, posture, and balance due to brain injury or abnormal
brain development.
Causes:  Genetic mutations, maternal infections, fetal stroke, brain hemorrhage, infant infections, trauma,
hypoxia
Risk Factors:
Maternal infections (CMV , rubella, herpes, syphilis, toxoplasmosis)
Infant illnesses (meningitis, encephalitis, jaundice)
Prematurity, low birth weight, multiple births, delivery complications
Signs and Symptoms:
Spasticity, muscle tone abnormalities, poor coordination, tremors
Motor delays, difficulty walking, speech and feeding problems
Seizures, vision/hearing impairment, bladder/bowel dysfunction
Types:
Spastic  (most common) – diplegia, hemiplegia, quadriplegia
Dyskinetic  – involuntary writhing or jerky movements
Ataxic  – balance and coordination problems
Mixed  – combination of above
Diagnosis:  Clinical evaluation, MRI, ultrasound, EEG
Complications:  Contractures,  malnutrition,  mental  health  issues,  respiratory  and  cardiac  problems,
osteoporosis
Nursing Care:
Promote mobility, prevent deformities
Nutritional support•

2. Down’s Syndrome (Trisomy 21)
Definition:  Genetic disorder caused by an extra chromosome 21.
Causes:
Chromosomal nondisjunction, translocations, mosaicism
Risk increases with maternal age and family history
Physical Characteristics:
Upward slanting eyes, small ears and mouth, flat nasal bridge, short neck
Single palmar crease, short stature, loose joints
Associated Conditions:  Congenital heart defects, intestinal abnormalities, vision/hearing problems, thyroid
and blood disorders
Diagnosis:
Prenatal screening (ultrasound, serum markers)
Cytogenetic testing
Management:
Surgical correction of anomalies
Balanced diet and exercise
Routine immunizations
Thyroid hormone replacement if needed
Supportive therapies and education
Nursing Care:

3. Mental Retardation (Intellectual Disability)
Definition:  Significant limitation in intellectual functioning and adaptive behavior originating before age 18.
Causes:
Genetic disorders (e.g., Down syndrome)
Head trauma
Prenatal exposures (alcohol, drugs, infections)
Illnesses (encephalitis, congenital hypothyroidism)
Toxic exposures (lead, mercury)
Signs and Symptoms:
Delayed speech and learning
Poor memory and academic performance
Behavioral problems including aggression, anxiety, impulsivity
Classification:
Mild (IQ 55-70)  – educable, majority of cases
Moderate (IQ 35-54)  – trainable, limited independence
Severe (IQ 20-34)  – extensive supervision needed
Profound (<20)  – maximal care and support required
Challenges:  Social isolation, bullying, low self-esteem, medical comorbidities
Diagnosis:  Developmental assessments, behavioral observations, family history
Prevention:
Avoidance of teratogens during pregnancy•
