Unit Four: Infectious Disorders
Neonatal Infections
Definition
Definition:  Infection in newborns (up to one month) due to immature immune systems.
Infection may be acquired antenatally, intranatally, or postnatally.
Modes of Infection
Antenatal:  transplacental, amnionitis
Intranatal:  early membrane rupture, vaginal contamination, cord sepsis
Postnatal:  transmission from mother, relatives, hospital staff, cross-infection, contaminated
feeding or bathing articles
Specific Neonatal Infections
1. Ophthalmia Neonatorum (Conjunctivitis):
Etiology: Neisseria gonorrhoeae  or Chlamydia  from infected birth canal
Symptoms:  red swollen eyelids, purulent discharge within 2-3 days of birth
Complications:  corneal ulceration, opacity, vision loss
Treatment:  penicillin, saline/boric acid eye cleaning, chloramphenicol drops
Nursing:  instill drops properly, restrain infant, protect unaffected eye, frequent irrigation
Prevention:  maternal infection control, prophylactic eye drops at birth•

2. Oral Moniliasis (Thrush):
Etiology: Candida albicans  acquired during delivery or postnatal from poor hygiene or
antibiotic use
Symptoms:  white oral patches causing bleeding, anorexia, poor suckling
Complications:  esophagitis, pneumonia, gastritis
Treatment:  nystatin drops, gentian violet, dektrin gel
Nursing:  individual feeding equipment, cleanliness, mouth care, daily inspection during
antibiotic therapy
3. Congenital Viral Infections (TORCH Syndrome):
Infectious agents:  Toxoplasma, Other (syphilis, varicella, HIV), Rubella, CMV, Herpes
simplex
Signs:  intrauterine growth retardation, thrombocytopenia, rash, microcephaly, jaundice,
hepatosplenomegaly, fever, poor feeding
Complications:  hearing impairment, growth retardation, mental retardation, autism,
death
Diagnosis:  maternal history, physical exam, blood cultures
Treatment:  antivirals/antibiotics, supportive care, close follow-up
Prevention:  maternal screening, immunization, hygiene, avoiding exposure to infectious
agents•

Postnatal Neonatal Infections
Impetigo:  Staphylococci or streptococci infection causing pustules and crusts; treated
with antibacterial soap, topical and systemic antibiotics; good prognosis with treatment.
Umbilical Infection:  Caused by E. coli  or Staphylococci; presents with redness, foul
odor; complications include septicemia and tetanus; treated with broad-spectrum
antibiotics and cord care; isolation recommended.
Tetanus Neonatorum: Clostridium tetani  infection causing muscle spasms, jaw
stiffness, respiratory distress; high mortality; treatment includes antitoxin, sedation,
respiratory support; prevention by DPT vaccination and aseptic cord care.
Neonatal Meningitis:  Inflammation of brain membranes; common pathogens include 
Group B Streptococcus , E. coli , others; symptoms include lethargy, seizures, bulging
fontanel; diagnosis by CSF analysis, PCR, imaging; treatment with antibiotics and
supportive care.
Nursing Care of Neonatal Infection:
Early recognition and monitoring
Isolation and infection control
Nutritional support
Prevent complications
Support parents emotionally and educate on precautions and care•
