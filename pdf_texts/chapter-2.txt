Unit One: An Overview of Human Growth and Development
Objectives
Define growth, maturation, and development.
Differentiate between growth and development.
Identify types, importance, and principles of growth and development.
Recognize patterns and stages of growth and development.
Enumerate factors influencing growth and development.
Introduction
Growth and development are multifaceted processes influenced by a variety of factors—some modifiable, others not.
Knowledge of these influences is crucial for healthcare providers, especially nurses, to guide parents in fostering optimal
child growth and development.
Definitions
Growth:  Quantitative increase in physical size due to cell multiplication in organs; measured by height,
weight, head and chest circumference.
Maturation:  Sequential biological changes that enable new abilities, reflecting the orderly development of
the child.
Development:  Qualitative progression in skills, functions, and social relations; a lifelong continuous process
from conception to maturity.
Comparison Between Growth and Development
Growth Development
Physical and physiological changes Continuous overall changes in individual
Quantitative changes Qualitative changes
Cellular multiplication Maturation plus environmental interaction
Part of the developmental process Broader, encompassing term
Types of Growth and Development
Physical Growth:  Monitored via head circumference, thoracic diameter, weight, height.
Physiological Growth:  Assessed through vital signs (temperature, pulse, respiration, BP).
Motor Development:  Includes milestones like sitting, standing, running, fine muscle use.
Mental/Intellectual Development:  Problem-solving abilities.
Social Development:  Cultural training and socialization.
Emotional/Personality Development:  Needs for trust, love, and security.•

Importance of Studying Growth and Development
Anticipate age-appropriate behaviors.
Understand child behavior and developmental norms.
Diagnose nutritional and deficiency disorders.
Plan comprehensive care.
Assist parents in adjusting the environment to support optimal growth.
Principles of Development
Continuous and sequential—each stage builds on the previous.
Predictable—occurs in a predetermined order influenced by genetics and environment.
Depends on maturation and learning.
Early development is more critical.
Individual differences exist in the rate but not the sequence.
Patterns of Growth and Development
Cephalocaudal:  Growth from head downward.
Proximodistal:  Development from the midline to extremities.
General to Specific:  Progression from gross to fine motor skills.
Stages of Growth and Development
Stage Age Range Characteristics
Prenatal Conception to
birthRapid cellular transformation from single cell to full human body.

development.

Adolescence 10 to 20 years Transition to adulthood, abstract reasoning, identity formation, autonomy.
Factors Influencing Growth and Development
Prenatal Factors
Hereditary/Genetics:  Parental traits influence physical characteristics (e.g., height).
Race/Nationality:  Variations in growth patterns among races.
Maternal Diseases:  Congenital rubella, metabolic disorders.
Drugs:  Teratogenic effects (e.g., amphetamines causing heart defects).
Nutrition:  Severe maternal malnutrition can impair fetal development.•

Postnatal Factors (External)
Environment:  Climate impacts growth rates; hot climates promote faster growth.
Seasonal variation:  Height grows faster in spring/summer; weight in autumn/winter.
Oxygen concentration:  Chronic hypoxia leads to smaller size.
Environmental hazards:  Trauma (e.g., poliomyelitis), socioeconomic status.
Postnatal Factors (Internal)
Endocrine:  Hormones like growth hormone, thyroid hormone, and androgens stimulate growth.
Gender:  Boys typically have greater muscle strength and activity levels.
Psychological:  Emotional and maternal deprivation affect weight and growth.
Diseases, Drugs, Nutrition:  Affect overall development.
Nurse’s Assessment of Growth and Development
Growth:  Measuring head and chest circumference, weight, height, and observing teeth eruption.
Development:  Observing motor, social, mental, emotional milestones, and sensory capabilities.•

Unit One: Newborn Stage
Learning Objectives
Define neonatal period.
List screening tests.
Describe physiological adaptations post-birth.
Explain heat loss and thermoregulation.
Identify normal newborn characteristics and needs.
Apply nursing care plans.
Recognize common neonatal problems.
Introduction
The neonatal period (birth to 28 days) is critical, with high mortality risk. During this time, the newborn undergoes
significant physiological adaptation from intrauterine to extrauterine life.
Newborn Screening Tests
Hearing screening:  Early detection of hearing impairments.
Blood tests:  Heel prick for metabolic and genetic disorders.
Oxygen screening:  Pulse oximetry to detect cardiac defects.
Physical Characteristics of Normal Newborn
Anthropometric Measurements
Length averages 49-50 cm.
Weight averages 2.7-3.85 kg; physiological weight loss of 5-10% occurs initially.
Vital Signs
Temperature: 36.1°C to 37.2°C; hands/feet may be cooler due to immature thermoregulation.
Pulse: 120-150 bpm, irregular with crying.
Respiration: 35-50 breaths/min, irregular rhythm.
Blood Pressure: Average 74/47 mmHg, transiently elevated with crying.
Skin Characteristics
Color varies with ethnicity; elasticity indicates hydration.
Lanugo hair present on premature infants.
Vernix caseosa covers skin variably, especially in folds.
Desquamation (skin peeling) in post-mature newborns.
Milia (white papules) common and transient.
Mongolian spots and capillary hemangiomas are benign.
Head and Face
Large head relative to body (1/4 body length).
Fontanels (anterior and posterior) palpable and gradually close.•

Caput succedaneum and cephalohematoma are transient birth injuries.
Facial features include soft ears, short neck, blue or gray eyes, and tearless crying initially.
Chest and Abdomen
Chest circumference slightly smaller than head.
Rounded abdomen with umbilical cord containing two arteries and one vein.
Adjustment to Extrauterine Life
Respiratory Changes
First breath inflates lungs; full expansion takes days.
Transition from placental oxygenation to pulmonary respiration.
Circulatory Changes
Closure of fetal shunts: foramen ovale, ductus arteriosus, and ductus venosus.
Increase in systemic and decrease in pulmonary pressures.
Other Systems
Thermoregulation relies on brown fat and metabolic activity; shivering absent.
Gastrointestinal tract matures near term; stomach capacity ~50-60 ml.
Urinary system immature with frequent voiding; genitalia show hormonal influences.
Musculoskeletal system flexible; neurological system immature with reflex dominance.
Reflexes
Protective reflexes include Moro, tonic neck, grasping, eye blinking.
Feeding reflexes: sucking, swallowing, gag.
Sensory development: touch highly developed; vision immature; hearing functional.
Basic Needs of Newborn
Clear airway and adequate respiration.
Thermal regulation.
Infection protection.
Love and attachment.
Nutrition.
Nursing Process Application
Assessment
Continuous monitoring with Apgar scoring at 1 and 5 minutes.
Vital signs and reflexes observed.
Intervention
Maintain airway and initiate respiration.
Prevent heat loss: drying, skin-to-skin contact, warming environment.
Infection control: hygiene, cord care, prophylactic eye drops, vitamin K.•

Unit One: Normal Growth and Development of Infant (1 Month to 1 Year)
Objectives
Define infancy.
Identify physical, motor, social, emotional, and cognitive development.
Enumerate infant needs and daily care practices.
Physical Growth
Weight gain: ~0.75 kg/month (birth to 4 months), decreasing later.
Length increases ~2.5 cm/month for first 6 months.
Head circumference grows ~1.5 cm/month initially.
Chest circumference equals head circumference by 1 year.
Dentition begins ~6 months; 10 teeth by 1 year.
Developmental Milestones
Sensory and Cognitive
Visual tracking by 2 months.
Hand-eye coordination develops by 4 months.
Taste preferences emerge.
Object permanence and problem-solving begin by 9 months.
Motor Skills
Head control by 2 months.
Sitting with support by 4 months.
Crawling by 9 months.
Walking with assistance by 10-12 months.
Social and Emotional Development
Responds to smiles, stranger anxiety appears.
Trust vs. mistrust crisis (Erikson).
Oral stage (Freud): gratification via mouth.
Speech milestones progress from cooing to simple words by 12 months.
Infant Needs
Love, security, nutrition, comfort, sensory stimulation.
Daily Care
Hygiene: clean eyes, nose, ears, mouth.
Diaper changes.
Proper carrying techniques (cradle, upright, football hold).
Safe bathing.•

Unit One: Normal Growth and Development of Toddler (1 to 3 Years)
Objectives
Define toddler stage.
Recognize physical, motor, social, cognitive, and emotional development.
Identify needs and common behavioral traits.
Physical Growth
Weight gain ~1.8 to 2.7 kg/year.
Height gain ~5 to 12 cm/year.
Head circumference increases marginally.
Chest circumference surpasses head circumference.
Physiological Parameters
Pulse: 98-140 bpm.
Respiration: 22-37 breaths/min.
Blood pressure: 86-106/42-63 mmHg.
Motor Development
15 months: walks alone, builds 2-3 block tower.
18 months: runs, climbs stairs with assistance.
24 months: walks stairs alone, imitates circle.
30 months: jumps, good hand coordination.
Language Development
15 months: 4-6 words.
18 months: 7-20 words.
24 months: 50 words, two-word sentences.
30 months: increasing vocabulary and sentence complexity.
Social and Emotional Development
Separation anxiety and temper tantrums.
Psychosocial crisis: autonomy vs. shame/doubt (Erikson).
Anal stage (Freud): focus on control of bodily functions.
Needs
Love and security.
Graded independence.
Toilet training readiness.
Language stimulation.
Healthy eating habits.•

Unit One: Normal Growth and Development of Pre-School Child (3 to 6 Years)
Objectives
Recognize growth, motor, mental, emotional development.
Understand psychosocial, psychosexual, cognitive development.
Identify needs and common problems.
Physical Growth
Weight gain: ~2.3 kg/year.
Height gain: ~6.25-7.5 cm/year.
Child appears slender, graceful by age 6.
Motor Skills
Fine motor: drawing, dressing, using scissors.
Gross motor: running, hopping, tricycle riding.
Cognitive Development (Piaget)
Preoperational phase.
Shift from egocentric to social awareness begins.
Language use expands rapidly.
Social Development
Overcomes stranger anxiety.
Learns to tolerate separation.
Language sophistication aids emotional expression.
Psychosexual (Freud)
Phallic stage; oedipal conflict resolves by late preschool.
Psychosocial (Erikson)
Initiative vs. guilt: child learns to initiate activities or feel guilty.
Needs
Security, independence, guidance.
Sex education appropriate to developmental level.
Language development.
Religious education.
Problems
Thumb-sucking, Enuresis and encopresis, Selfishness and temper tantrums, Masturbation, Destructiveness.•

Unit One: Normal Growth and Development of School-Age Child (6 to 12 Years)
Objectives
Define school-age period.
Discuss physical, physiological, cognitive, psychosocial development.
Identify health problems and needs.
Physical Growth
Slow, steady growth.
Weight gain ~3 kg/year; height gain ~6 cm/year.
Boys taller and heavier; girls have more fatty tissue.
Permanent teeth eruption begins.
Physiological Development
Mature gastrointestinal, genitourinary, circulatory, neurological systems.
Improved posture encouraged.
Endocrine system mature except reproductive functions.
Psychosexual (Freud)
Latency period: sexual energy dormant; focused on socialization.
Psychosocial (Erikson)
Industry vs. inferiority: mastering skills vs. feelings of inadequacy.
Cognitive Development (Piaget)
Concrete operational stage.
Logical thinking, conservation, and classification develop.
Social and Moral Development
Increasing peer interaction.
Moral reasoning evolves from rigid to cooperative.
Needs
Sleep, nutrition, exercise, dental health, education (including sex and religious).
Health Problems
School phobia, learning difficulties, Behavioral issues (lying, stealing), Nutritional problems (obesity, underfeeding),
Communicable diseases, Accidents and bullying.•

Unit One: Normal Growth and Development of Adolescence (12 to 18 Years)
Objectives
Define adolescence and puberty.
Describe physical, cognitive, psychosocial, psychosexual development.
Identify common physical and psychological problems.
Discuss adolescent needs.
Definitions
Adolescence:  transitional period from childhood to adulthood (WHO: 10-19 years).
Puberty:  period of rapid growth and sexual maturation.
Physical Changes
Growth spurts; secondary sexual characteristics develop.
Girls: breast development, menstruation, pelvic changes.
Boys: genital enlargement, voice changes, spermatogenesis.
Factors Affecting Growth
Heredity, sex, environment, nutrition, socioeconomic status, illness, hormonal balance.
Cognitive Development (Piaget)
Formal operational stage: abstract thinking, problem-solving.
Psychosocial Development (Erikson)
Identity vs. role confusion: developing personal identity.
Psychosexual Development (Freud)
Genital stage: focus on sexual relationships; conflict between desires and societal norms.
Emotional Changes
Heightened emotionality due to physical changes, social expectations, hormonal activity, family conflicts.
Health Problems
Premenstrual syndrome, dysmenorrhea, Concerns about body image, Masturbation issues, Postural defects, Fatigue,
anemia, Acne vulgaris, Obesity, Food faddism, Hyperthyroidism, Delinquency, drug abuse, severe psychoneurosis,
suicide.
Needs
Personality integration.
Emancipation from family.•
